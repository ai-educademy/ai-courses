# ⚖️ AI Ethics in Interviews

You've just nailed the coding round and the system design question. Then the interviewer leans forward and asks, "How would you ensure your model doesn't discriminate against protected groups?" The room goes quiet. If you don't have a structured way to think about AI ethics, this question can derail an otherwise excellent interview. Here's the good news: ethics questions aren't about having perfect answers — they're about demonstrating that you think carefully about the impact of the systems you build.

---

## 🤔 Why Companies Ask About AI Ethics

This isn't a box-ticking exercise. Companies ask about ethics for very practical reasons:

- **Regulatory pressure**: the EU AI Act, NYC's Local Law 144 (bias audits for hiring algorithms), and GDPR all impose real compliance requirements
- **Reputation risk**: one biased model can generate headlines that cost millions in brand damage
- **Legal liability**: discriminatory AI outputs can lead to lawsuits
- **User trust**: users are increasingly aware of — and sensitive to — algorithmic unfairness
- **Talent retention**: engineers want to work at companies that take responsible AI seriously

<Callout>
Don't treat ethics questions as a "soft" afterthought. The strongest candidates weave ethical thinking naturally into their technical answers rather than treating it as a separate topic.
</Callout>

## 🔍 Types of Bias in AI Systems

Understanding bias taxonomy is the foundation of every ethics discussion.

### Data Bias

The data used to train the model doesn't represent the real world fairly.

- **Historical bias**: training data reflects past discrimination. A hiring model trained on historical decisions learns to prefer candidates that look like people who were previously hired — perpetuating existing demographic imbalances.
- **Representation bias**: certain groups are underrepresented in the data. A facial recognition system trained mostly on light-skinned faces performs poorly on darker-skinned faces.
- **Measurement bias**: the way data is collected systematically distorts it. Crime prediction models trained on arrest data reflect policing patterns, not actual crime rates.

### Algorithmic Bias

The model amplifies or introduces bias through its learning process.

- **Proxy variables**: even if you remove protected attributes (race, gender), the model may use correlated features (zip code, name) as proxies
- **Feedback loops**: a model's predictions influence future data collection. A content moderation system that over-flags content from certain communities generates more "violations" for those communities, reinforcing the pattern
- **Optimisation bias**: the objective function itself may favour certain outcomes — maximising overall accuracy often sacrifices minority group performance

### Societal Bias

Broader systemic inequalities that are reflected in and reinforced by AI systems.

- **Automation bias**: humans over-trust AI outputs, especially when presented with high confidence scores
- **Confirmation bias**: developers test models against their own expectations, missing failure modes that affect different populations

## 📏 Fairness Metrics and Trade-Offs

This is where ethics gets mathematical — and where interview answers become truly impressive.

### Key Fairness Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Intuition |
|---|---|---|
| **Demographic parity** | P(Ŷ=1 \| A=0) = P(Ŷ=1 \| A=1) | Equal positive prediction rates across groups |
| **Equalised odds** | Equal TPR and FPR across groups | Equal accuracy for each group |
| **Predictive parity** | Equal precision across groups | Positive predictions are equally reliable |
| **Individual fairness** | Similar individuals get similar predictions | Requires a distance metric on individuals |

### The Impossibility Theorem

Here's a critical insight for interviews: **you cannot simultaneously satisfy all fairness metrics** (except in trivial cases). This is known as the impossibility theorem of fairness.

For example, if base rates differ between groups (Group A has 5% positive rate, Group B has 15%), you cannot achieve both demographic parity and equalised odds simultaneously.

```
Group A: 1,000 people, 50 positive (5% base rate)
Group B: 1,000 people, 150 positive (15% base rate)

Demographic parity demands: equal prediction rates
Equalised odds demands: equal TPR and FPR

If base rates differ → these two goals conflict mathematically
```

## 🔦 Explainability and Transparency

If you can't explain why your model made a decision, how can you verify it's fair?

### Model-Level Explainability

- **Inherently interpretable models**: linear regression, decision trees, logistic regression — you can inspect the weights directly
- **Post-hoc explanation methods**: SHAP, LIME, attention visualisation — explain individual predictions of complex models

### SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

Based on game theory, SHAP assigns each feature an importance value for each individual prediction:

```python
import shap

explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(model)
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)

# Visualise feature importance for a single prediction
shap.force_plot(explainer.expected_value, shap_values[0], X_test.iloc[0])
```

### The Right to Explanation

Under GDPR's Article 22, individuals have the right to "meaningful information about the logic involved" in automated decisions that significantly affect them. This means explainability isn't optional for many real-world applications.

<FunFact>
In 2019, Apple Card was accused of gender discrimination after several users reported that the algorithm offered wives significantly lower credit limits than their husbands — despite shared finances. An investigation by the New York Department of Financial Services found that the model wasn't intentionally discriminatory, but its lack of transparency made it impossible to quickly diagnose and address the disparity.
</FunFact>

## 🏛️ Responsible AI Frameworks

Companies and governments are building structured approaches to responsible AI:

### Key Principles (Common Across Frameworks)

1. **Fairness**: systems should treat all people equitably
2. **Transparency**: stakeholders should understand how decisions are made
3. **Privacy**: personal data should be protected throughout the ML pipeline
4. **Safety**: systems should be robust and fail gracefully
5. **Accountability**: clear ownership for AI system outcomes
6. **Human oversight**: meaningful human control over high-stakes decisions

### Practical Implementation

- **Bias audits**: regularly test models across demographic groups before and after deployment
- **Model cards**: document model purpose, performance across groups, intended use, and limitations
- **Datasheets for datasets**: document data collection process, composition, and known biases
- **Red-teaming**: adversarial testing specifically targeting fairness and safety

## 📖 Real-World Case Studies

Interviewers love case studies because they test your ability to analyse real situations, not just recite principles.

### Facial Recognition Bias

A landmark study by Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru found that commercial facial recognition systems had error rates of:
- **0.8%** for light-skinned males
- **34.7%** for dark-skinned females

The cause was a combination of representation bias (training data skewed towards light-skinned faces) and evaluation bias (benchmarks didn't test across demographic groups).

### Hiring Algorithm Bias

Amazon built a hiring algorithm trained on 10 years of résumé data. The model learned to penalise résumés containing the word "women's" (e.g., "women's chess club") because the historical data reflected a male-dominated tech workforce. The project was eventually scrapped.

### Content Moderation

Automated content moderation systems have been shown to disproportionately flag African American Vernacular English (AAVE) as toxic, because training datasets labelled AAVE expressions as offensive at higher rates than Standard American English.

## 💡 How to Structure Your Ethics Answers

Use this framework when answering ethics questions in interviews:

1. **Acknowledge the concern**: show you take it seriously ("This is an important consideration because...")
2. **Identify specific risks**: name the types of bias or harm that could occur
3. **Propose technical mitigations**: bias audits, fairness constraints, explainability tools
4. **Discuss trade-offs honestly**: acknowledge that fairness constraints may reduce overall accuracy
5. **Mention process safeguards**: model cards, human review, monitoring, escalation paths
6. **Acknowledge limitations**: be honest about what technology can and can't solve

**Example response pattern**:

> "For a credit scoring model, I'd be concerned about historical bias in lending data disproportionately affecting minority applicants. I'd start by auditing model performance across demographic groups using equalised odds and demographic parity metrics. If disparities exist, I'd explore fairness-aware training, such as adding fairness constraints to the optimisation objective. However, I'd be transparent that improving fairness for underserved groups may slightly reduce overall AUC, and I'd present that trade-off to stakeholders. Post-deployment, I'd monitor prediction distributions by demographic group and set up alerts for divergence."

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

- **Ethics questions test your judgement**, not just your knowledge — show you think beyond accuracy
- **Know the bias taxonomy**: data bias, algorithmic bias, societal bias — and give concrete examples
- **Fairness has mathematical trade-offs**: you can't satisfy all fairness metrics simultaneously (impossibility theorem)
- **Explainability is both a technical tool and a legal requirement** — know SHAP, LIME, and when interpretability matters most
- **Use real case studies**: facial recognition, hiring algorithms, and content moderation are well-documented examples
- **Structure your answers**: acknowledge → identify risks → propose mitigations → discuss trade-offs → mention processes → acknowledge limitations

<Callout>
The candidates who stand out on ethics questions don't just list problems — they propose solutions while honestly acknowledging that some problems don't have perfect answers. That intellectual honesty is exactly what interviewers are looking for.
</Callout>
