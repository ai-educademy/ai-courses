# 💬 NLP and LLM Interview Topics

Two years ago, NLP interview questions meant explaining word embeddings and LSTM vanishing gradients. Today, the interviewer is just as likely to ask, "When would you fine-tune a model versus use RAG?" The NLP landscape has shifted dramatically, and interviews have shifted with it. This lesson covers both the fundamentals interviewers still test *and* the LLM-era topics that now dominate the conversation.

---

## 📚 NLP Fundamentals Interviewers Still Expect

Even in the age of GPT, you need to demonstrate you understand *why* these models work, not just *that* they work.

### Tokenisation

Tokenisation converts raw text into discrete units the model can process. The approach matters:

- **Word-level**: simple but can't handle out-of-vocabulary words ("unfriendliness" → unknown)
- **Character-level**: handles any word but sequences are very long
- **Subword (BPE, WordPiece, SentencePiece)**: the modern standard — breaks rare words into meaningful pieces

```
"unfriendliness" → ["un", "friend", "li", "ness"]  (BPE)
"playing"        → ["play", "##ing"]                (WordPiece)
```

<Callout>
Interview tip: if asked about tokenisation, mention that tokenisation choices affect vocabulary size, sequence length, and the model's ability to handle rare or misspelt words. This shows you understand the downstream implications, not just the mechanics.
</Callout>

### Embeddings

Embeddings map discrete tokens to dense continuous vectors where semantic relationships are preserved:

- **Word2Vec / GloVe**: static embeddings — each word gets one vector regardless of context ("bank" has the same embedding whether it means a river bank or a financial institution)
- **Contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT)**: each token's embedding depends on surrounding context — "bank" gets different representations in different sentences

### The Attention Mechanism

Attention is the key innovation that makes transformers work. At its core:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

In plain English: for each token, attention computes a weighted average over all other tokens, where the weights reflect how relevant each token is to the current one. The scaling factor √d_k prevents the dot products from growing too large.

**Self-attention** allows every token to attend to every other token in the sequence, capturing long-range dependencies that RNNs struggle with.

### Transformer Architecture

The transformer consists of:
- **Encoder**: processes the full input (used in BERT-style models for understanding)
- **Decoder**: generates output token by token (used in GPT-style models for generation)
- **Encoder-Decoder**: combines both (used in T5, original transformer for translation)

| Architecture | Examples | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Encoder-only | BERT, RoBERTa | Classification, NER, embeddings |
| Decoder-only | GPT-4, LLaMA, Mistral | Text generation, chat, reasoning |
| Encoder-Decoder | T5, BART | Translation, summarisation |

## 🤖 LLM-Specific Interview Questions

These are the questions that have emerged in the last two years and now appear in nearly every ML interview.

### Fine-Tuning vs Prompting

| Approach | When to Use | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Zero/Few-shot prompting** | Quick prototyping, general tasks | No training needed, flexible | Limited control, costs per query |
| **Prompt engineering** | Moderate customisation | Fast iteration, no GPU costs | Fragile, hard to version |
| **Fine-tuning (full)** | Domain-specific tasks with labelled data | Best quality, smaller model possible | Expensive, needs data and GPU |
| **LoRA / QLoRA** | Fine-tuning with limited resources | Efficient, small adapter weights | Slightly less expressive than full fine-tuning |
| **RAG** | Knowledge-intensive, up-to-date info needed | No retraining, citable sources | Retrieval quality bottleneck |

### Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

RAG combines a retrieval system with a language model:

```
User query → Embed query → Search vector DB → Retrieve top-K documents
    → Construct prompt (query + retrieved docs) → LLM generates answer
```

**Key interview discussion points**:
- **Chunking strategy**: how you split documents affects retrieval quality
- **Embedding model choice**: domain-specific vs general embeddings
- **Reranking**: use a cross-encoder to rerank retrieved chunks before feeding to the LLM
- **When RAG fails**: when the answer requires reasoning across many documents, or when no relevant document exists

### RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)

The technique behind ChatGPT's helpfulness:

1. **Supervised fine-tuning**: train on curated prompt-response pairs
2. **Reward model training**: humans rank model outputs; train a reward model on these preferences
3. **PPO optimisation**: use the reward model to fine-tune the LLM via reinforcement learning

<FunFact>
The original InstructGPT paper showed that a 1.3B parameter model with RLHF was preferred by humans over a 175B parameter model without it. Alignment techniques can make small models outperform giant ones on real-world usefulness.
</FunFact>

### Context Windows and Long-Context Challenges

- **Context window**: the maximum number of tokens a model can process at once (e.g., GPT-4 Turbo: 128K, Claude: 200K)
- **Lost in the middle**: models tend to pay less attention to information in the middle of long contexts
- **Strategies**: summarise long documents, place critical information at the beginning/end, use hierarchical retrieval

### Hallucination

Models generate plausible but factually incorrect text. Mitigation strategies:
- **RAG**: ground responses in retrieved evidence
- **Constrained generation**: limit output to known entities or structured formats
- **Self-consistency**: generate multiple answers and check agreement
- **Citation**: require the model to cite its sources (then verify them)

A legal technology company wants to build an AI assistant that answers questions about case law. Hallucination could lead to citing non-existent legal precedents. How would you architect this system to minimise hallucination risk while keeping responses useful?
</ThinkAboutIt>

## 🎯 Model Selection: When to Use What

This is a favourite interview question: "Given this use case, which model would you choose?"

**Use a large frontier model (GPT-4, Claude) when:**
- Task requires general reasoning or complex instruction following
- You have no labelled training data
- Prototyping speed matters more than inference cost

**Use a fine-tuned smaller model (Mistral 7B, LLaMA 3) when:**
- You have domain-specific data and need consistent, specialised outputs
- Inference cost matters (serving at scale)
- Latency requirements are tight
- You need to run on-premises for data privacy

**Use a traditional NLP model (BERT, RoBERTa) when:**
- The task is classification, NER, or extraction (not generation)
- You need fast inference at very high throughput
- The task is well-defined with clear labelled data

## 🔬 Evaluating LLM Outputs

Evaluating generative models is harder than evaluating classifiers. Common approaches:

- **Automated metrics**: BLEU, ROUGE, BERTScore (useful but imperfect)
- **LLM-as-judge**: use a stronger model to evaluate a weaker model's outputs
- **Human evaluation**: gold standard but expensive and slow
- **Task-specific evaluation**: accuracy on QA benchmarks, code execution pass rates
- **Red-teaming**: adversarial testing for safety, bias, and harmful outputs

```python
# BERTScore example
from bert_score import score

precision, recall, f1 = score(
    cands=["The cat sat on the mat"],
    refs=["A cat was sitting on a mat"],
    lang="en"
)
```

## ⚖️ Ethical Considerations

NLP interviews increasingly include ethics questions:

- **Bias in training data**: LLMs absorb and amplify biases from their training corpora
- **Privacy**: models can memorise and regurgitate training data (PII leakage)
- **Misinformation**: generative models can create convincing but false content at scale
- **Environmental cost**: training large models has a significant carbon footprint

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

- **Know your fundamentals**: tokenisation, embeddings, attention, and transformer architecture are non-negotiable
- **Understand the fine-tuning spectrum**: from zero-shot prompting to full fine-tuning, and when each is appropriate
- **RAG is the most practical LLM pattern** — understand chunking, retrieval, and reranking
- **Model selection is about trade-offs**: capability vs cost vs latency vs privacy
- **Evaluation of generative models is an open problem** — know multiple approaches
- **Ethics isn't optional**: bias, hallucination, and privacy are now standard interview topics

The NLP field is moving incredibly fast. Before your interview, check what models and techniques have been released in the last three months. Being able to discuss recent developments (while understanding the fundamentals) signals that you're actively engaged in the field.
</Callout>
