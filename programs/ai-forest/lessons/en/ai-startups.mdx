# AI Startups and Business Models

The AI gold rush is real - but most startups fail not because of bad technology, but because of bad business models. The difference between a successful AI company and a failed one rarely comes down to model quality. It comes down to distribution, positioning, and unit economics. This lesson covers what separates AI companies that thrive from those that burn through funding and vanish.

## AI Business Models That Actually Work

Not every AI company looks the same, and picking the wrong business model is a common reason AI startups fail despite having strong technology. Here are the dominant models that have proven to work:

**SaaS with AI Inside** - Traditional software enhanced by AI. Think Grammarly or Notion AI. Users pay a subscription; AI is the differentiator, not the entire product. This model works well because users are already familiar with SaaS pricing.

**API-as-a-Service** - Sell model access via API. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Cohere all follow this pattern. Revenue scales with usage, but margins depend on inference costs.

**Vertical AI Platforms** - Purpose-built for one industry. Harvey (legal), Viz.ai (radiology), and AlphaSense (finance) dominate because generic tools simply cannot match their domain depth. These companies win by understanding their users better than any horizontal competitor can.

**Marketplace / Aggregator** - Platforms like Hugging Face or Replicate that host others' models and take a cut. Network effects create powerful moats that strengthen over time.

**AI Consulting / Services** - Custom AI solutions for enterprises. High margins per project but hard to scale without productising. Many successful SaaS companies started here, using consulting engagements to learn customer needs before building a product.

## Finding Your Niche

The biggest mistake first-time AI founders make is building horizontal tools that compete directly with Big Tech. You will not out-resource Google or OpenAI. Instead, go vertical and go deep.

**Vertical AI** targets a specific industry with deep domain expertise. A legal AI tool trained on millions of case law documents beats a general-purpose LLM for contract review every time, because it understands the nuances that generic models miss.

**How to pick your vertical:**
1. **Talk to practitioners** - find workflows that are painful, repetitive, and high-value. Spend at least 20 hours interviewing before writing any code.
2. **Check data availability** - can you access domain-specific training data legally and ethically?
3. **Assess regulation** - regulated industries (healthcare, finance) have higher barriers to entry but also higher willingness to pay and stronger lock-in
4. **Size the market** - a £50M niche is perfectly fine if you can realistically capture 20% of it
5. **Evaluate competition** - are incumbents already solving this with AI, or is the field wide open?

## Building Your MVP

Your first version should be embarrassingly simple. The goal is to validate demand, not showcase technical brilliance. Most failed AI startups spent too long building before talking to customers.

**The 3-week MVP framework:**
- **Week 1:** Build a wrapper around an existing model (GPT-4, Claude, or an open model like Llama 3). Add domain-specific prompting, a system prompt tailored to your use case, and a simple UI.
- **Week 2:** Get it in front of 10 real users from your target audience. Watch them use it. Take notes on every point of confusion and frustration.
- **Week 3:** Iterate on the top 3 pain points. Ignore everything else - focus is survival.

Do not fine-tune a model before you have paying users. Do not build custom infrastructure before you have product-market fit. Do not hire a large ML team before you know what to build. Premature optimisation kills AI startups faster than bad models.

<Callout>
**Rule of thumb:** If you can solve 80% of the problem with prompt engineering and RAG, do that first. Fine-tuning and custom models come later when you have data and revenue to justify the investment.
</Callout>

## The Funding Landscape

AI startups attract significant venture capital, but investors have become more discerning since the hype peak of 2023. The era of funding pitch decks with "we use AI" is over - now you need substance.

**What investors look for:**

Securing funding requires more than a demo - you need to demonstrate a credible path to a sustainable business.

- **Proprietary data** - do you have access to data competitors cannot easily replicate?
- **Technical differentiation** - are you doing something beyond wrapping an API? Fine-tuned models, custom architectures, or novel data pipelines count here.
- **Revenue traction** - even £5K MRR signals real demand and validates product-market fit
- **Team-market fit** - founders with domain expertise outperform pure technologists in vertical AI
- **Clear unit economics** - inference costs must leave room for healthy gross margins (aim for 70%+)
- **Defensible roadmap** - a credible plan for building moats over 12-24 months

<FunFact>
The median AI startup that raised a Series A in 2024 had just 18 months of runway remaining and £800K in annual recurring revenue. Investors increasingly expect revenue before writing large cheques.
</FunFact>

## Competitive Moats in AI

Building defensibility is the most important strategic challenge for any AI startup. Without a moat, a well-funded competitor can replicate your product in months. Here are the moats that actually matter:

| Moat | Strength | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Proprietary data** | Very strong | Bloomberg's financial data for BloombergGPT |
| **Distribution** | Strong | Microsoft embedding Copilot across Office |
| **Domain expertise** | Strong | Viz.ai's clinical validation in radiology |
| **User-generated data** | Growing | Each user interaction improves the product |
| **Switching costs** | Moderate | Deep workflow integration makes migration painful |

A model alone is never a moat. Models are commoditising rapidly - what was cutting-edge six months ago is often freely available today. Your moat comes from everything around the model: the data you feed it, the workflows you embed it in, and the relationships you build with customers.

## Case Studies Worth Studying

Real-world examples teach more than theory ever can. These startups illustrate both what works and what can go wrong.

**Jasper** - Built a content marketing AI tool on top of GPT-3. Grew to £80M ARR before competition from ChatGPT forced a pivot. Lesson: API wrappers are vulnerable without deeper moats.

**Harvey** - AI for lawyers, founded by an ex-lawyer and an ML engineer. Raised $100M+ by combining deep legal domain expertise with cutting-edge LLM technology. Lesson: team-market fit matters enormously - the lawyer co-founder was critical to product design and sales.

**Glean** - Enterprise search powered by AI. Won by integrating deeply into company workflows (Slack, Confluence, Drive). Lesson: distribution and integration create stickiness that competitors cannot easily replicate.

## Key Takeaways

The AI startup landscape rewards speed, focus, and strategic thinking over technical perfection. Remember these principles:

- Choose a business model that matches your strengths and market
- Go vertical before going horizontal - depth beats breadth
- Build the simplest possible MVP and validate with real users
- Moats come from data, distribution, and domain expertise - never from models alone
- Revenue traction speaks louder than technical demos to investors
- Study both successes and failures - failed startups teach you what to avoid

The best time to start an AI company is when you have deep domain knowledge, access to unique data, and a clear problem that existing solutions handle poorly. Technology alone is never enough - execution and timing matter just as much.
