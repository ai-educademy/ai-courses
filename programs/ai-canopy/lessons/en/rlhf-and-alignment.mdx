# RLHF and Alignment - How ChatGPT Learns Human Values

A pre-trained language model is a powerful but uncontrolled engine. It can write poetry, generate code, and reason about physics - but it can also produce toxic content, confidently fabricate facts, and ignore user instructions. **Alignment** is the process of steering that raw capability towards being genuinely helpful, honest, and harmless.

## The Alignment Problem

Pre-training optimises a single objective: **predict the next token**. This gives the model broad knowledge but no understanding of what a human actually *wants*. Ask a base model a question and it might complete the text as if writing a forum post, an advertisement, or a conspiracy theory - because all of those patterns exist in its training data.

The gap between "capable" and "useful" is the alignment problem. A model that can write medical advice but doesn't know when to refuse is potentially dangerous. Closing this gap requires teaching the model **human preferences** - not just what *could* come next, but what *should* come next.

## Stage 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

The first step is straightforward: collect thousands of high-quality **demonstration examples** - ideal conversations between a user and an assistant - and fine-tune the base model on them. This teaches the model the *format* of being helpful: answer questions directly, follow instructions, admit uncertainty, and refuse harmful requests.

SFT alone produces a noticeably better model, but it has limitations. Human demonstrators can only write so many examples, and the model learns to imitate the *average* quality of those demonstrations rather than consistently producing the best possible response.

## Stage 2: Reward Model Training

Instead of writing ideal responses, human labellers are given **pairs of model outputs** and asked to rank which is better. This is cognitively easier - comparing is simpler than creating - and allows collecting far more preference data.

These ranked pairs train a **reward model (RM)**: a separate neural network that takes a prompt and response as input and outputs a scalar score representing human preference. The RM learns patterns like:

- Accurate, sourced responses score higher than vague ones
- Responses that acknowledge uncertainty score higher than confident fabrications
- Helpful refusals score higher than complying with harmful requests

## Stage 3: Reinforcement Learning (PPO)

With a reward model in hand, the SFT model is further optimised using **Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO)**. The process:

1. The model generates a response to a prompt
2. The reward model scores the response
3. PPO updates the model weights to increase the expected reward
4. A **KL divergence penalty** prevents the model from drifting too far from the SFT baseline (avoiding reward hacking)

The KL penalty is critical. Without it, the model would learn to exploit quirks of the reward model - generating responses that score highly but are nonsensical or repetitive. By constraining how far the policy can diverge from the SFT model, PPO keeps outputs coherent while improving alignment.

This three-stage process - SFT → Reward Model → PPO - is precisely how OpenAI transformed GPT-3.5 into ChatGPT, as described in their **InstructGPT** paper.

<FunFact>
The InstructGPT paper revealed a striking result: a 1.3B parameter model aligned with RLHF was preferred by human judges over the unaligned 175B GPT-3 - a model 100× larger. Alignment can matter more than raw scale.
</FunFact>

## DPO: Cutting Out the Middleman

**Direct Preference Optimisation (DPO)** simplifies the RLHF pipeline by eliminating the separate reward model entirely. Instead, DPO frames alignment as a classification problem: given a preferred response and a rejected response, directly update the language model's weights to increase the probability of the preferred one.

The DPO loss function implicitly defines a reward model within the language model itself:

```
L_DPO = -log σ(β · (log π(y_w|x)/π_ref(y_w|x) - log π(y_l|x)/π_ref(y_l|x)))
```

Where `y_w` is the preferred response, `y_l` is the rejected one, and `π_ref` is the reference (SFT) model. DPO is simpler, more stable, and cheaper to train - no reward model, no RL loop, no hyperparameter-sensitive PPO. Meta's LLaMA 2 and many open-source models now use DPO or its variants.

## Constitutional AI: Self-Supervision at Scale

Anthropic introduced **Constitutional AI (CAI)** to reduce reliance on human labellers. The approach:

1. Define a set of principles (a "constitution") - e.g., *"Choose the response that is most helpful while being honest and harmless"*
2. Generate response pairs and use the **model itself** to judge which response better adheres to the constitution
3. Use these AI-generated preferences to train the reward model (RLAIF - RL from AI Feedback)

CAI scales preference collection dramatically - the AI can generate and evaluate millions of comparisons at a fraction of the cost of human labelling. It also makes the alignment criteria **explicit and auditable**, encoded in the constitution rather than implicitly learned from labeller biases.

## Red Teaming

No alignment technique is perfect. **Red teaming** involves deliberately trying to make the model produce harmful, biased, or untruthful outputs. Red teamers probe for:

- Jailbreaks that bypass safety training
- Systematic biases in responses across demographics
- Factual errors in high-stakes domains (medical, legal)
- Inconsistencies in refusal boundaries

Major labs now conduct extensive red teaming before releasing models, often involving external security researchers, domain experts, and dedicated adversarial testing teams.

## The Alignment Tax

Alignment comes at a cost. RLHF-trained models sometimes become **overly cautious** - refusing benign requests, adding unnecessary caveats, or producing bland responses to avoid any perceived risk. This "alignment tax" is a real engineering challenge:

- Too little alignment → harmful outputs
- Too much alignment → unhelpful, annoying refusals

The frontier of alignment research is finding techniques that maximise helpfulness while maintaining genuine safety boundaries - not just pattern-matching on superficially "safe" responses.

<FunFact>
OpenAI spent roughly six months on alignment work (SFT data collection, reward modelling, and RLHF training) to transform GPT-4's base model into the version released to the public. The alignment phase, while a fraction of the pre-training compute, required thousands of hours of specialised human labelling.
</FunFact>
