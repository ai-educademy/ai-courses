# Scaling Laws and Training at Scale - Why Bigger Models Get Smarter

The modern AI revolution is not just an algorithmic story - it is an **engineering and scaling** story. Researchers discovered that model performance follows predictable power laws across parameters, data, and compute, and exploiting those laws required inventing entirely new distributed training paradigms.

## Kaplan Scaling Laws

In 2020, OpenAI researchers (Kaplan et al.) published a landmark finding: Transformer loss follows **smooth power-law relationships** with three variables:

- **N** - Number of model parameters
- **D** - Dataset size (tokens)
- **C** - Compute budget (FLOPs)

The key result: `L(N) ∝ N^(-0.076)` - loss decreases as a power law of parameter count. Double the parameters, and loss drops predictably. These curves showed no sign of plateauing across six orders of magnitude, suggesting that scaling was a reliable path to better performance.

Kaplan's recommendation: when compute is limited, it is more efficient to **train a larger model for fewer steps** than a smaller model for more steps. This guided the decision to scale GPT-3 to 175 billion parameters.

## Chinchilla and Compute-Optimal Training

In 2022, DeepMind's **Chinchilla paper** challenged Kaplan's conclusions. Their key insight: the optimal allocation between parameters and training tokens is roughly **1:20** - for every parameter, you should train on approximately 20 tokens.

| Model | Parameters | Training Tokens | Ratio |
|-------|-----------|----------------|-------|
| GPT-3 | 175B | 300B | 1.7:1 |
| Chinchilla | 70B | 1.4T | 20:1 |
| LLaMA 2 70B | 70B | 2T | 28:1 |

Chinchilla (70B parameters) **outperformed** the much larger Gopher (280B) by training on 4× more data. The lesson was profound: most large models were massively **over-parameterised and under-trained**.

## Why Parameter Count Is Not Everything

A model's capability depends on the interplay of architecture, data, and training. Consider:

- **Data quality** matters more than raw volume. Models trained on curated, deduplicated, high-quality text consistently outperform those trained on larger but noisier datasets. Meta's LLaMA showed that a 13B model trained on carefully curated data could match GPT-3 (175B) on many benchmarks.
- **Architecture choices** - attention patterns, activation functions (SwiGLU vs ReLU), normalisation placement (pre-norm vs post-norm) - all shift the scaling curves.
- **Tokenisation** affects effective data volume. A better tokeniser compresses more information per token, changing the parameter-to-token ratio.

## Distributed Training Strategies

No single GPU can hold a frontier model. Training requires distributing work across thousands of accelerators using several complementary strategies:

**Data Parallelism** - The simplest approach: replicate the entire model on each GPU, split the training batch across devices, and average gradients after each step. Works well when the model fits in a single GPU's memory.

**Model Parallelism (Tensor Parallelism)** - Split individual layers across GPUs. A single matrix multiplication is divided so each GPU computes a slice of the output. Requires high-bandwidth interconnects (NVLink, InfiniBand) because GPUs must communicate within every forward/backward pass.

**Pipeline Parallelism** - Assign different layers to different GPUs, passing activations forward and gradients backward through the pipeline. Introduces **pipeline bubbles** (idle time while waiting for other stages), mitigated by micro-batching.

Modern systems combine all three: tensor parallelism within a node (fast NVLink), pipeline parallelism across nodes, and data parallelism across node groups.

## Mixed Precision and Gradient Accumulation

**Mixed precision training** uses half-precision floats (FP16 or BF16) for most computations while keeping a master copy of weights in FP32. Benefits:

- **2× memory reduction** for activations and gradients
- **2-8× throughput increase** on modern GPUs with tensor cores
- BF16 (Brain Float 16) is preferred over FP16 because its wider exponent range avoids overflow issues common in FP16 training

**Gradient accumulation** simulates large batch sizes without proportional memory cost: run multiple forward/backward passes, accumulate gradients, and only update weights after N micro-steps. This lets you achieve effective batch sizes of millions of tokens even on limited hardware.

## DeepSpeed and FSDP

Two frameworks dominate distributed training:

- **DeepSpeed (Microsoft)** - Implements ZeRO (Zero Redundancy Optimiser) stages 1-3, progressively sharding optimiser states, gradients, and parameters across GPUs. ZeRO-3 can train trillion-parameter models by ensuring no single GPU holds a full copy of anything.
- **FSDP (PyTorch)** - Fully Sharded Data Parallel, PyTorch's native equivalent to ZeRO-3. Integrated directly into the PyTorch ecosystem, making it more accessible for researchers.

## Emergent Abilities and Diminishing Returns

As models scale, certain capabilities appear to **emerge suddenly** rather than improving gradually. Examples include:

- Multi-step arithmetic appearing around 100B parameters
- Chain-of-thought reasoning improving dramatically at scale
- Few-shot learning becoming reliable only above certain thresholds

However, recent research debates whether emergence is real or an artefact of **discontinuous evaluation metrics**. When using continuous metrics (like per-token log-probability), performance often improves smoothly - the "sudden jump" is an illusion of the measurement, not the model.

The **diminishing returns debate** is also intensifying. Each order of magnitude in compute yields diminishing improvements in benchmarks, leading some researchers to argue that scaling alone will not achieve artificial general intelligence without fundamental architectural innovations.
