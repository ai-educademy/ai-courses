# Deep Neural Networks - CNNs, RNNs, and Transformers Explained

Three architectures have shaped modern deep learning: **CNNs** for spatial data, **RNNs** for sequences, and **Transformers** for nearly everything. Understanding their strengths and trade-offs is essential for any advanced AI practitioner.

## Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

CNNs were designed for **spatial data** - images, video frames, and anything with a grid-like structure. They work by sliding small filters across the input to detect patterns.

The genius of CNNs lies in two properties:
- **Parameter sharing** - The same filter is applied across the entire image, drastically reducing the number of weights compared to fully connected layers.
- **Local connectivity** - Each neuron only connects to a small region of the input, reflecting the fact that nearby pixels are more related than distant ones.

**Core operations:**
1. **Convolution** - A small filter (e.g., 3×3) slides across the image, computing a dot product at each position. Each filter learns to detect a specific feature - edges, textures, or shapes.
2. **Pooling** - Reduces spatial dimensions by taking the maximum or average value in small regions. This makes the representation more compact and somewhat invariant to small shifts.
3. **Feature maps** - Each convolutional layer produces multiple feature maps, building from simple edge detectors in early layers to complex object recognisers in deeper layers.

A typical CNN for image classification stacks several convolutional and pooling layers, then flattens the result and passes it through fully connected layers for the final prediction.

**Landmark CNN architectures:**
- **LeNet-5** (1998) - One of the earliest CNNs, used for handwritten digit recognition.
- **AlexNet** (2012) - Won ImageNet and reignited interest in deep learning. Used ReLU activations and dropout.
- **ResNet** (2015) - Introduced residual (skip) connections, enabling networks with 150+ layers. This idea of "shortcut connections" later influenced Transformer design.
- **EfficientNet** (2019) - Systematically scales depth, width, and resolution for optimal performance per compute budget.

<FunFact>
The architecture of CNNs was inspired by the visual cortex of cats. In the 1960s, Hubel and Wiesel discovered that neurons in a cat's brain respond to specific visual patterns in specific regions of the visual field - almost exactly how convolutional filters work.
</FunFact>

## Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and LSTMs

RNNs were designed for **sequential data** - text, speech, time series, and anything where order matters. The key idea: the network maintains a **hidden state** that gets updated at each time step, creating a form of memory.

At each step, the RNN takes two inputs: the current token and the previous hidden state. It produces a new hidden state that encodes everything the network has "seen" so far. In theory, this gives RNNs unlimited memory. In practice, the story is more complicated.

**The vanishing gradient problem:** In practice, standard RNNs struggle with long sequences. During backpropagation through many time steps, gradients either shrink to near zero (vanishing) or explode to infinity, making it difficult for the network to learn long-range dependencies.

To understand why this matters, consider a sentence like: *"The author, who grew up in Paris and later moved to London before settling in New York, wrote..."* An RNN must carry the information about "author" through dozens of intervening tokens to correctly predict "wrote." In practice, this signal fades long before it reaches the verb.

**LSTMs (Long Short-Term Memory)** solve this with a gating mechanism:
- **Forget gate** - Decides what information to discard from the cell state.
- **Input gate** - Decides what new information to store.
- **Output gate** - Decides what to output based on the current cell state.

These gates allow LSTMs to selectively remember or forget information across hundreds of time steps, making them far more effective than vanilla RNNs.

**GRUs (Gated Recurrent Units)** are a simplified variant of LSTMs with only two gates (reset and update) instead of three. They often achieve similar performance with fewer parameters, making them a popular choice when computational resources are limited.

<Callout>
Although RNNs and LSTMs have been largely superseded by Transformers for most NLP tasks, they remain useful in edge computing and real-time applications where model size and latency constraints rule out large Transformer models.
</Callout>

## Transformers - The Architecture Behind Everything

The Transformer, introduced in 2017, replaced recurrence with **self-attention**, allowing the model to look at every token in the input simultaneously.

**Key components:**

**Self-attention** - Each token computes attention scores with every other token. This means a word at position 1 can directly attend to a word at position 1000, without information passing through intermediate states.

**Positional encoding** - Since Transformers process all tokens in parallel (no inherent sense of order), positional information is injected by adding sine and cosine functions of different frequencies to the input embeddings.

**Multi-head attention** - Instead of a single attention computation, the model runs several in parallel. Each "head" can learn to attend to different types of relationships - one might capture syntax, another captures coreference, another captures semantic similarity.

**Feed-forward layers** - After attention, each token passes through a small neural network independently. This is where much of the model's "knowledge" is stored.

**Layer normalisation and residual connections** - These stabilise training and allow gradients to flow through very deep networks (modern LLMs have 80+ layers).

The encoder-decoder structure of the original Transformer has since been split into specialised variants:
- **Encoder-only** (e.g., BERT) - Excellent for understanding tasks like classification and named entity recognition.
- **Decoder-only** (e.g., GPT, Llama) - Designed for text generation, predicting one token at a time.
- **Encoder-decoder** (e.g., T5, BART) - Best for sequence-to-sequence tasks like translation and summarisation.

## Why Transformers Won

| Property | CNN | RNN/LSTM | Transformer |
|----------|-----|----------|-------------|
| **Parallelisation** | High | Low (sequential) | High |
| **Long-range dependencies** | Limited by kernel size | Difficult (vanishing gradients) | Excellent (direct attention) |
| **Training efficiency** | Good | Poor at scale | Excellent at scale |
| **Versatility** | Spatial data | Sequential data | Nearly universal |

The Transformer's ability to **parallelise** training across GPUs was the decisive advantage. RNNs must process tokens one by one, making them painfully slow on modern hardware. Transformers process the entire sequence at once, enabling training on billions of tokens in reasonable time.

Furthermore, self-attention captures **long-range dependencies** directly. An RNN must carry information through hundreds of hidden states; a Transformer simply attends to the relevant token regardless of distance.

## Modern Developments

Transformers now dominate not just language but also:
- **Vision** - Vision Transformers (ViT) match or exceed CNNs on image tasks.
- **Audio** - Whisper and other models transcribe speech using Transformer encoders.
- **Multimodal** - Models like GPT-4o process text, images, and audio within a single Transformer.
- **Biology** - AlphaFold uses attention mechanisms to predict protein structures.

The boundaries between these architectures are blurring. Modern "CNN" models often include attention layers, and Transformers sometimes borrow convolutional ideas for efficiency.

**State Space Models (SSMs)**, such as Mamba, represent an emerging alternative that processes sequences in linear time rather than quadratic. While still early, they hint that the Transformer's dominance may not be permanent - the search for better architectures continues.

The lesson from this history is clear: no architecture is final. Each breakthrough - from CNNs to RNNs to Transformers - solved problems its predecessors couldn't. The next paradigm shift may already be taking shape in research labs today.

Understanding all three architectures gives you the conceptual vocabulary to evaluate new ideas as they emerge - and to choose the right tool for each problem you face.
