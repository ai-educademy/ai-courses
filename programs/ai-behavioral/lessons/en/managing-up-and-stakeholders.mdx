# Managing Up and Stakeholders — The Advanced Skill That Unlocks Staff+ Roles

At the Staff and Principal engineer level, your ability to manage relationships *upward* — with directors, VPs, and C-suite executives — becomes as important as your technical depth. These are the hardest behavioural questions to prepare for because they require a level of organisational sophistication that most engineers have never been coached on.

If you are interviewing for a senior, Staff, or leadership-track role, expect at least two or three questions about influencing without authority, delivering difficult messages, and managing stakeholder expectations. This lesson prepares you for all of them.

## What "Managing Up" Means in Interview Context

"Managing up" is not about flattering your boss. It is about **proactively shaping the information flow, expectations, and decisions** that happen above your level. It means:

- Ensuring your leadership has the right context to make good decisions
- Surfacing risks early instead of hoping they resolve themselves
- Pushing back when you believe a direction is wrong — with data and alternatives
- Managing expectations so that surprises are minimised

<Callout>
At the Staff+ level, interviewers expect you to be the person who tells leadership what they *need* to hear, not what they *want* to hear. Stories about comfortable agreement are not what they are looking for.
</Callout>

## The Five Question Types

### 1. Influencing Without Authority

"Tell me about a time you convinced leadership to change direction."

This is the most common "managing up" question. The signal they want: you can build a compelling case, navigate organisational dynamics, and drive a decision without relying on positional power.

### 2. Delivering Bad News

"Describe a time you had to tell leadership about a significant problem."

The signal: you surface problems early, take ownership, and present solutions alongside the bad news.

### 3. Pushing Back on Unrealistic Expectations

"Tell me about a time you pushed back on a deadline or scope from leadership."

The signal: you protect your team from unsustainable commitments while maintaining the trust of senior stakeholders.

### 4. Managing Multiple Stakeholders

"Describe a situation where different stakeholders wanted conflicting things."

The signal: you can negotiate, align, and find paths forward when priorities collide at the leadership level.

### 5. Executive Communication

"How do you communicate technical decisions to non-technical executives?"

The signal: you can translate complexity into clear business language and adjust your communication style to your audience.

## Framework: The ISED Model for Influencing Up

When telling stories about influencing leadership, structure your Action section using **ISED**:

- **Identify** the misalignment or risk before it becomes a crisis
- **Substantiate** your position with data, prototypes, or external evidence
- **Engage** the right stakeholders — timing and audience matter
- **Drive** to a decision, even if it is not your preferred outcome

## Example Story: Influencing Without Authority

> **Situation:** Our engineering VP announced a plan to migrate all services to a new cloud provider within six months as part of a cost-reduction initiative. Based on my experience running our most complex services, I believed the timeline was unrealistic and the migration would cause significant production instability.
>
> **Task:** I needed to influence the VP to adjust the plan without appearing to undermine a decision that had already been announced.
>
> **Action:** I started by doing my homework. I mapped our 47 services by migration complexity — simple stateless services, stateful services with database dependencies, and critical-path services with real-time SLAs. I categorised them into three tiers and estimated realistic timelines for each. I requested a one-to-one with the VP and framed my input as "helping refine the execution plan" rather than opposing the decision. I presented the tiered approach: migrate Tier 1 services in months one to three (quick wins that demonstrate progress), Tier 2 in months four to eight, and Tier 3 in months nine to twelve with a dedicated stability budget.
>
> I also quantified the risk of the original plan — based on industry data, compressed cloud migrations of our scale had a 70% probability of major incidents. I proposed measuring success by migration quality, not just speed.
>
> **Result:** The VP adopted the tiered approach and extended the timeline to twelve months. We completed the migration with zero customer-impacting incidents. The VP later cited the tiered framework in a company-wide presentation as a model for large technical initiatives. More importantly, I established myself as someone leadership could trust for honest, data-backed counsel.

## Example Story: Delivering Bad News

> **Situation:** Three weeks before a major product launch, our load testing revealed that the payment processing system could not handle the projected Black Friday traffic — we would hit capacity at 60% of expected peak.
>
> **Task:** I needed to inform the product director and CTO about a problem that could delay or compromise the launch.
>
> **Action:** I refused to sugarcoat the situation. I prepared a one-page brief with three sections: the problem (with data), the impact (projected revenue loss if we launched without fixing it), and three options with trade-offs. Option A: delay launch by two weeks and fix properly. Option B: launch with a traffic cap and queue system. Option C: launch as planned and accept the risk. I scheduled a meeting the same day — I did not wait for the weekly sync.
>
> In the meeting, I led with the impact number, not the technical details. "We risk losing £1.2 million in Black Friday revenue if we launch without changes. Here are three paths forward." The CTO chose Option B, and I led the implementation of the queue system over the next ten days.
>
> **Result:** We launched on time with the queue system. The system handled 140% of projected traffic with no downtime. The product director later told me that the structured brief — problem, impact, options — was the clearest escalation she had ever received from engineering. I now use this format for every significant risk escalation.

<ThinkAboutIt>
Think about the last time you knew about a risk but hesitated to raise it. What held you back? Fear of being wrong? Fear of being the messenger? The ability to raise uncomfortable truths early is what separates Staff engineers from senior engineers.
</ThinkAboutIt>

## Pushing Back on Unrealistic Deadlines

This is one of the most important and delicate skills to demonstrate. The key is pushing back **with alternatives**, not just resistance.

**What bad pushback looks like:**
- "That timeline is not realistic." (No data, no alternatives)
- "The team cannot do it." (Sounds like you are protecting comfort, not quality)
- "We tried something like this before and it failed." (Defeatist without constructive input)

**What good pushback looks like:**
- "We can hit that date if we reduce scope to X. Here is what I would cut and why."
- "The full scope needs eight weeks. I can deliver the highest-impact 70% in five weeks and the rest in a follow-up sprint."
- "The deadline is achievable, but it requires borrowing an engineer from Team B. Here is the trade-off for their roadmap."

## Managing Multiple Stakeholders

When stakeholders conflict, your role is to be **the translator and the honest broker** — someone who represents the trade-offs clearly without taking political sides.

**Principles for multi-stakeholder navigation:**
1. **Understand each stakeholder's core need** — not their stated request, but the underlying business goal
2. **Find the overlap** — there is almost always shared ground beneath surface-level conflicts
3. **Make trade-offs explicit** — put options on paper where everyone can see them
4. **Propose, do not just present** — come with a recommendation, not just a menu

## Executive Communication Tips

For Staff+ interviews, you may be asked to demonstrate executive communication directly. Key principles:

- **Lead with the decision or recommendation**, not the analysis: "I recommend we delay by two weeks. Here is why."
- **Use business language**: revenue impact, customer impact, risk probability — not technical jargon
- **Be concise**: executives operate on compressed information. One page beats ten slides
- **Anticipate questions**: prepare for "what if we do nothing?" and "what is the worst case?"

<ThinkAboutIt>
Imagine your CTO asks you for a status update on a struggling project. Can you explain it in three sentences — what is happening, what is the impact, and what you recommend — without using any technical terms? Practice this skill regularly.
</ThinkAboutIt>

## Key Takeaways

- **Managing up is a professional skill**, not politics. It means shaping the information and decisions above your level.
- **Use the ISED model**: Identify the misalignment, Substantiate with data, Engage the right people, Drive to a decision.
- **Deliver bad news early, structured, and with options.** Problem → Impact → Options is the gold standard format.
- **Push back with alternatives**, never with bare resistance. Show what *is* possible, not just what is not.
- **Translate technical complexity into business language.** Revenue, risk, and timeline are the currency of executive conversations.

The engineers who reach Staff+ and beyond are the ones leadership trusts with uncomfortable truths and hard trade-offs. Build that trust, and the promotions follow.
