# Natural Language Processing - How AI Understands Human Language

You have probably asked a voice assistant a question, used Google Translate, or watched YouTube auto-captions appear in real time. Behind every one of those experiences is **Natural Language Processing (NLP)** - the branch of AI that helps machines work with human language.

## Why Language is Hard for Machines

Language seems effortless to us, yet it is spectacularly messy for computers:

- **Ambiguity** - "I saw her duck" - did she crouch, or did you see her pet bird?
- **Context** - "That's sick!" can mean something is disgusting or something is brilliant,
  depending entirely on who is speaking and when.
- **Sarcasm** - "Oh great, another Monday" carries the opposite of its literal meaning, but
  there is no explicit marker to signal this.
- **Structure** - the same words rearranged can change meaning entirely: "Dog bites man" versus
  "Man bites dog."
- **Evolving slang** - new words and meanings emerge constantly, making static dictionaries
  insufficient.

Machines cannot rely on intuition or life experience the way humans do. NLP systems must learn
patterns from enormous volumes of text, often billions of sentences.

## Core NLP Tasks

| Task | What it does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Tokenisation** | Splits text into words or sub-words | "I'm learning" → ["I", "'m", "learning"] |
| **Sentiment analysis** | Detects emotion or opinion | "This film was brilliant!" → Positive |
| **Named entity recognition** | Finds names, places, dates | "Marie Curie worked in Paris" → Person, Location |
| **Machine translation** | Converts between languages | English → French |
| **Summarisation** | Condenses long text | A 10-page report → a short paragraph |

## Turning Words into Numbers

Machines work with numbers, not words. So how do you represent the richness of language
mathematically? The breakthrough insight was representing words as **vectors** - lists of numbers
that capture meaning and relationships.

**Word2Vec** (2013) showed that words used in similar contexts get similar vectors. Famously, the maths worked out so that:

> *king* − *man* + *woman* ≈ *queen*

This proved that word embeddings capture genuine semantic relationships.

**BERT** (2018) went further by making embeddings **contextual** - the vector for "bank" changes depending on whether the sentence is about rivers or finance.

<FunFact>
BERT was trained on the entire English Wikipedia and the BookCorpus - roughly 3.3 billion words. Yet the model itself has "only" 340 million parameters, meaning it compresses a vast library of human knowledge into patterns far smaller than the original text.
</FunFact>

## How ChatGPT Works (The Short Version)

ChatGPT is built on a **transformer** architecture - the same family of models behind Google
Translate, GitHub Copilot, and many other AI tools. Here is the simplified pipeline:

1. **Tokenise** your message into sub-word pieces.
2. Use **attention mechanisms** to figure out which words relate to which - even across very long
   passages with thousands of tokens.
3. Predict the next word, one token at a time, based on everything that came before it.
4. The model was fine-tuned using **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)** - real
   people rated outputs to teach the model helpfulness, accuracy, and safety.

The "attention" idea is the magic ingredient. It lets the model focus on the most relevant parts
of the input, no matter how far apart they appear in the text. Before transformers, models
struggled with long documents because earlier words faded from memory.

## Real-World Applications

- **Spam filters** - Gmail uses NLP to block over 10 million spam emails every minute.
- **Chatbots** - customer service bots handle routine queries, freeing human agents for complex problems.
- **Search engines** - Google's search now understands full questions, not just keyword matches.
- **Legal tech** - AI reviews thousands of legal documents in hours, a task that once took lawyers weeks.
- **Accessibility** - real-time captioning helps deaf and hard-of-hearing users follow live events.

<FunFact>
Google Translate processes over 100 billion words per day across 133 languages. Before neural NLP, it relied on phrase tables and produced famously awkward translations. Modern transformer-based translation is remarkably fluent.
</FunFact>

## Limitations and Challenges

Despite remarkable progress, NLP is far from a solved problem:

- **Hallucinations** - language models can generate confident-sounding text that is factually
  wrong. They have no built-in way to verify truth.
- **Bias** - models trained on internet text absorb societal biases present in that data,
  sometimes producing sexist, racist, or otherwise harmful outputs.
- **Low-resource languages** - NLP works best for English; many of the world's 7,000+ languages
  have very little digital text for training.
- **Privacy** - processing personal messages, emails, and documents raises serious data protection
  concerns under regulations like GDPR.
- **Energy cost** - training large language models requires enormous computational resources,
  raising environmental sustainability questions.

## Key Takeaways

- Human language is full of ambiguity, context, slang, and nuance - making it one of AI's hardest
  challenges.
- Word embeddings convert language into mathematical vectors that capture genuine semantic
  relationships between words.
- Transformers and attention mechanisms power today's most capable language models, from ChatGPT
  to Google Search.
- NLP drives everyday tools including search engines, spam filters, chatbots, and translation
  services.
- Hallucinations, bias, energy costs, and data privacy remain significant open problems that
  researchers and companies are actively working to solve.
