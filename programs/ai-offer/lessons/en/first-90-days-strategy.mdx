# First 90 Days Strategy — From New Hire to Trusted Contributor

You have negotiated the offer, signed the contract, and cleared your notice period. Day one arrives — and suddenly, you are the person who knows the least in the room. Every system is unfamiliar, every acronym is new, and your Slack is full of channels you do not understand yet. This moment is simultaneously the most exciting and the most fragile point in your career. What you do in your first 90 days determines whether you become a trusted contributor or spend six months treading water. The good news? There is a proven framework for this.

<Callout>
Your first 90 days are not about proving you are smart. They are about proving you are someone people want to work with — and that you can ship.
</Callout>

## The 30-60-90 Day Framework

The 30-60-90 framework divides your first three months into three phases with distinct goals:

| Phase | Timeline | Primary Goal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Learn** | Days 1–30 | Absorb context, build relationships, understand the landscape |
| **Contribute** | Days 31–60 | Deliver your first meaningful contributions and deepen relationships |
| **Own** | Days 61–90 | Take ownership of a workstream, establish your impact, and get feedback |

Each phase builds on the previous one. Trying to skip to "Own" in week two is the fastest way to alienate your new team. Let us break each phase down.

## Phase 1: Learn (Days 1–30)

### Weeks 1–2: Listen, Observe, and Connect

Your only job in the first two weeks is to learn. Resist the urge to "add value" immediately. Instead, focus on:

**Meet everyone you can.** Schedule 30-minute 1:1s with every team member, your skip-level manager, and key stakeholders from adjacent teams. Use a simple template:

> "Hi [name], I am [your name] — just joined the [team name] team. I would love to grab 30 minutes to learn about what you work on and how our teams interact. What does your typical week look like?"

In these conversations, ask:
- What is the team's biggest challenge right now?
- What do you wish someone had told you when you joined?
- Who else should I talk to?

**Understand the codebase.** Do not try to read every file. Instead:
- Find the main entry points and trace a single request end-to-end
- Read the README files (if they exist), architecture decision records, and recent pull requests
- Set up the development environment and run the test suite locally
- Identify the areas of the code that change most frequently — that is where your work will likely be

**Identify quick wins.** As you explore, keep a running list of small improvements you notice — outdated documentation, flaky tests, missing error handling, confusing variable names. These become your first contributions.

<ThinkAboutIt>
Think about your last role. What did you wish you had known in the first week? What context would have saved you from your first mistake? Use that experience to guide what you look for in your new role.
</ThinkAboutIt>

### Weeks 3–4: Your First Contribution

By the end of week two, you should have enough context to pick up a small, well-defined task. The ideal first contribution is:

- **Small enough** to complete in 2-3 days
- **Visible enough** that the team notices
- **Low risk** — if you get it wrong, nothing breaks in production
- **Valuable** — it solves a real problem, not a made-up one

Good examples: fixing a bug that has been in the backlog for weeks, improving a CI pipeline step, adding test coverage to an under-tested module, or updating stale documentation.

<FunFact>
Research from Microsoft's engineering productivity team found that new hires who submitted their first pull request within the first week had significantly higher retention rates and faster promotion timelines than those who waited longer. Early momentum matters.
</FunFact>

**How to approach your first PR:**

1. Pick a task and tell your manager: "I would like to take on [task]. Does that seem like the right size and priority?"
2. Read any existing patterns or coding standards before writing code
3. Keep the PR small — under 200 lines if possible
4. Write a thorough PR description explaining your approach and any trade-offs
5. Request reviews from 2-3 team members (this builds relationships)

## Phase 2: Contribute (Days 31–60)

### Build Deeper Relationships

By month two, you should shift from "meeting everyone" to building meaningful working relationships. Focus on:

**Find a mentor.** This does not have to be formal. Look for a senior engineer who is patient, generous with their time, and has deep institutional knowledge. Ask them to pair with you on complex tasks.

**Understand team dynamics.** Every team has unwritten rules — who makes the real decisions, which meetings actually matter, how conflict is handled. Observe carefully and adjust your approach.

**Join cross-functional efforts.** Volunteer for a working group, a hiring committee, or a cross-team project. This expands your network beyond your immediate team and makes you visible to other leaders.

### Deliver Consistently

In month two, you should be picking up medium-sized tasks independently. The key is consistency over heroics:

- Ship 2-3 PRs per week rather than one massive PR every two weeks
- Ask questions early when you are stuck — do not disappear for three days
- Start participating in code reviews (this is one of the fastest ways to learn the codebase and build trust)
- Attend sprint ceremonies actively — ask clarifying questions, offer estimates, flag risks

<Callout>
Code reviews are a superpower for new hires. Reviewing others' code teaches you patterns, conventions, and domain knowledge faster than any onboarding document. Start reviewing early, even if your comments are just questions.
</Callout>

### Navigate the Codebase with Purpose

By now, you should have a mental model of the system architecture. Deepen it by:

- Drawing a diagram of the services, data flows, and dependencies (and sharing it — your fresh perspective is valuable)
- Understanding the deployment pipeline end-to-end
- Learning where the on-call runbooks are and reading through them
- Identifying the team's technical debt and understanding why it exists

## Phase 3: Own (Days 61–90)

### Take Ownership of a Workstream

By month three, you should be ready to own something meaningful. This might be:

- Leading the implementation of a feature from design to production
- Owning the reliability of a specific service
- Driving a technical improvement (migration, performance optimisation, observability)
- Running a spike and presenting your findings to the team

The key word is **ownership** — not just writing code, but being the person the team relies on for that area. This means tracking progress, communicating status, unblocking yourself, and escalating when needed.

### Share Knowledge

One of the most impactful things you can do in month three is share what you have learned:

- **Write documentation** for the things that were hardest to figure out — you are the best person to write it because the pain is still fresh
- **Give a short tech talk** or lightning talk about something you learned (5-10 minutes at a team meeting is enough)
- **Update onboarding docs** for the next person who joins

### Get Structured Feedback

Do not wait for your first formal review. Proactively schedule a feedback conversation with your manager around the 90-day mark:

> "I have been here for about three months now, and I would love to get your honest feedback. What am I doing well? Where should I focus on improving? Is there anything I should be doing differently?"

This accomplishes three things: it shows self-awareness, it gives you actionable direction, and it builds trust with your manager.

## Common Mistakes New Hires Make

### 1. Trying to Change Everything Too Fast
You might see inefficiencies everywhere — and you might be right. But proposing sweeping changes in your first month signals that you do not respect the context behind existing decisions. **Learn first, then propose.**

### 2. Going Dark When Stuck
New hires often feel pressure to figure everything out alone. This is counterproductive. Spending 30 minutes stuck is fine. Spending three days stuck without asking for help is a red flag to your manager.

### 3. Comparing Everything to Your Last Company
"At my previous company, we did it this way" is the fastest way to annoy your new colleagues. Instead, ask: "I am curious about the reasoning behind this approach — could you help me understand the context?"

### 4. Ignoring Non-Technical Relationships
Building relationships with product managers, designers, and stakeholders is just as important as impressing your fellow engineers. Software is a team sport.

<ThinkAboutIt>
Think about a time you saw a new joiner integrate really well into a team. What did they do that made them effective? Conversely, think about a new joiner who struggled — what would you have advised them to do differently?
</ThinkAboutIt>

## When to Speak Up vs Stay Quiet

This is one of the hardest judgement calls for new hires. Here is a practical guide:

**Speak up when:**
- You see a genuine bug or security issue
- You have direct, relevant experience with the problem being discussed
- Someone asks for your opinion or feedback
- A decision is being made that affects your work and you have a clear perspective

**Stay quiet (or ask a question instead) when:**
- You do not have enough context to form a meaningful opinion
- The discussion is about historical decisions you do not understand yet
- You want to show off rather than genuinely contribute
- The topic is politically sensitive and you do not yet understand the dynamics

The default for your first 30 days should be **asking questions** rather than making statements. "Could you help me understand why we chose this approach?" is almost always better than "I think we should do it differently."

## Building Trust with Your New Manager

Your relationship with your direct manager is the single most important relationship in your first 90 days. Here is how to build it:

- **Be reliable.** If you say you will do something, do it. If you cannot, communicate early.
- **Communicate proactively.** Send a brief weekly update (3-5 bullet points) summarising what you worked on, what you plan to do next, and any blockers. This reduces your manager's anxiety and makes their job easier.
- **Ask for their expectations explicitly.** "What does a successful first quarter look like to you?" removes ambiguity.
- **Be honest about what you do not know.** Faking confidence is transparent and erodes trust. Saying "I have not worked with Kafka before, but I am going to spend this week learning it" builds trust.

<FunFact>
A Harvard Business Review study found that the strongest predictor of a new hire's long-term success was not their technical skill but the quality of their relationship with their direct manager in the first 90 days. Investing in this relationship is not optional — it is strategic.
</FunFact>

## Key Takeaways

- **Use the 30-60-90 framework** — Learn, Contribute, Own. Each phase has a distinct purpose; do not skip ahead.
- **Prioritise relationships over code** in your first two weeks — 1:1 meetings are more valuable than pull requests right now.
- **Deliver a quick win early** — something small, visible, and valuable by the end of week three.
- **Ask questions freely** in month one, start forming opinions in month two, and own decisions in month three.
- **Avoid the classic mistakes** — do not try to change everything, do not go dark, and do not compare everything to your last job.
- **Build trust with your manager** through reliability, proactive communication, and honest self-awareness.
- **Request feedback at 90 days** — do not wait for a formal review cycle.
