# Evaluating Multiple Offers — A Framework for Career-Defining Decisions

You have done the hard work — multiple rounds of interviews, take-home assignments, system design sessions — and now you are holding two or three offer letters. Congratulations. But here is where most engineers make a critical mistake: they pick the offer with the biggest number on it and call it a day. The reality is that the highest salary is not always the best offer. The best offer is the one that maximises your **career trajectory**, your **daily happiness**, and your **long-term wealth** — and those things do not always live in the same spreadsheet cell.

## Why the Highest Salary Is Not Always the Best Offer

Imagine two offers. Offer A pays £95,000 at a large, stable company with a well-known brand. Offer B pays £82,000 at a fast-growing Series B startup with a small engineering team. On paper, Offer A wins by £13,000 a year. But Offer B gives you equity worth potentially £200,000 in four years, a direct reporting line to the CTO, and the chance to build a payments platform from scratch.

Three years later, the engineer who took Offer B has "led the architecture of a platform processing £50M annually" on their CV, while the engineer at Company A is still rotating through feature teams. Context matters more than the number.

<Callout>
A job offer is not just a salary — it is a bet on your future self. Evaluate where you will be in 3 years, not just where you will be on payday.
</Callout>

## Building a Weighted Decision Matrix

Gut feelings are unreliable when you are excited (or anxious) about multiple offers. Instead, use a structured **weighted decision matrix**. Here is how it works:

### Step 1: Define Your Criteria

Choose the factors that matter most to **you**. Common criteria for engineers include:

| Criteria | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Total Compensation** | Base salary + bonus + equity + benefits |
| **Growth Potential** | Promotion speed, scope of responsibility, learning curve |
| **Team & Manager Quality** | Engineering culture, manager's reputation, team size |
| **Work-Life Balance** | Hours, on-call expectations, flexibility, PTO |
| **Technical Challenge** | Interesting problems, modern stack, scale of systems |
| **Company Trajectory** | Revenue growth, funding stage, market position |
| **Location & Remote Policy** | Commute, remote/hybrid options, relocation needs |

### Step 2: Assign Weights (Must Total 100)

Not every factor matters equally. A senior engineer with a family might weight work-life balance at 25 and technical challenge at 10, while an early-career engineer might do the opposite. Be honest about what you actually value — not what you think you should value.

### Step 3: Score Each Offer (1–10) and Multiply

Here is an example comparing three real-ish offers:

| Criteria | Weight | Offer A (Big Bank) | Offer B (Startup) | Offer C (Scale-up) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Total Compensation | 25 | 9 (225) | 6 (150) | 8 (200) |
| Growth Potential | 20 | 5 (100) | 9 (180) | 8 (160) |
| Team & Manager | 20 | 6 (120) | 8 (160) | 9 (180) |
| Work-Life Balance | 15 | 7 (105) | 5 (75) | 7 (105) |
| Technical Challenge | 10 | 4 (40) | 9 (90) | 7 (70) |
| Company Trajectory | 10 | 7 (70) | 7 (70) | 9 (90) |
| **Total** | **100** | **660** | **725** | **805** |

In this scenario, Offer C — which was neither the highest paying nor the most exciting on paper — wins because it scores consistently well across all dimensions.

<ThinkAboutIt>
If you are currently evaluating offers, create your own matrix right now. What are your top 3 criteria, and how would you weight them? You might be surprised by what you actually prioritise when forced to assign numbers.
</ThinkAboutIt>

## Evaluating Equity Realistically

Equity is where most engineers get it wrong. A startup offering "£200,000 in stock options" sounds amazing until you understand the details.

### Startup Equity — Questions to Ask

- **What is the current valuation, and what was the last funding round?** This tells you what your shares are theoretically worth today.
- **What is the strike price?** If the strike price is close to the current fair market value, your upside is limited unless the company grows significantly.
- **What is the vesting schedule?** The standard is 4 years with a 1-year cliff, but some companies front-load or back-load vesting.
- **What is the liquidation preference?** In an acquisition, investors often get paid first. Your common shares might be worth less than you think.
- **What are the realistic exit scenarios?** IPO, acquisition, or staying private for 10 years? Each has very different implications for your equity.

### Public Company Equity

RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) at a public company are much simpler — they vest and you can sell them immediately. But watch out for:

- **Stock price volatility** — that £50,000 RSU grant is worth whatever the stock price is on the vesting date, not what it was when you signed.
- **Refresh grants** — does the company give annual equity refreshes? Without them, your total compensation drops significantly after Year 1.

<FunFact>
According to data from Levels.fyi, equity makes up 30-60% of total compensation at major tech companies like Google, Meta, and Amazon for senior engineers. Ignoring equity when comparing offers means ignoring potentially the largest component of your pay.
</FunFact>

## The Manager Factor — The Most Underrated Variable

Research from Gallup consistently shows that **people leave managers, not companies**. Yet most candidates spend zero time evaluating their future manager during the offer evaluation process.

Here is what to do:

- **Ask to have a 1:1 conversation with your future manager** before accepting. Any good company will facilitate this.
- **Ask them directly**: "What does success look like in the first 6 months?" and "How do you handle disagreements with your reports?"
- **Search for them on LinkedIn** — look at their career trajectory, how long their reports stay on their team, and whether former reports have progressed in their careers.
- **Ask your future peers** (if you met any during interviews): "What is it like working with [manager's name]?"

<Callout>
A great manager at a mediocre company will accelerate your career faster than a mediocre manager at a great company. Do not underestimate this factor.
</Callout>

## Location and Remote Work Considerations

The shift to remote work has fundamentally changed how we evaluate offers. Consider:

- **Fully remote** offers flexibility but can limit visibility and promotion speed at some companies.
- **Hybrid** (2-3 days in office) is becoming the norm — evaluate whether the commute is sustainable long-term.
- **Geographic pay adjustments** — some companies pay the same regardless of location; others adjust for local cost of living. A £90,000 offer with location-adjusted pay in Manchester might give you more purchasing power than £110,000 in London.
- **Tax implications** — working across state or country borders can create complicated tax situations.

## When to Walk Away from All Offers

Sometimes the right decision is to decline everything. Consider walking away if:

- None of the offers align with your long-term career goals
- You have a bad feeling about every manager or team you would join
- The offers are significantly below market rate and negotiation has failed
- You are choosing out of fear rather than excitement
- Your current role is actually better than all available options (it happens)

Walking away takes courage, but accepting a bad offer costs you 1-2 years of your career — time you cannot get back.

## A Real-World Example: Comparing Three Offers

Let us walk through a practical scenario. Priya, a mid-level backend engineer with 4 years of experience, received three offers:

- **Offer A (Big Bank)**: £95K base, £10K bonus, solid pension, stable but slow-moving, Java monolith
- **Offer B (Series B Startup)**: £78K base, £180K equity (4-year vest), small team, greenfield Go microservices, high growth
- **Offer C (Scale-up, 500 employees)**: £88K base, £15K bonus, £40K RSUs, modern stack, strong engineering culture, hybrid remote

Priya's priorities were growth potential (she wanted to reach senior in 2 years) and technical challenge (she was bored of CRUD apps). After building her matrix and talking to each future manager, she chose **Offer C** — not the highest cash compensation, but the role where her manager had a track record of promoting engineers, the technical problems were genuinely complex, and the company was growing fast enough to create new leadership opportunities.

## Key Takeaways

- **Build a weighted decision matrix** — it forces you to confront what you actually value and removes emotional bias from a high-stakes decision.
- **Evaluate equity with scepticism** — ask hard questions about valuation, vesting, liquidation preferences, and realistic exit timelines.
- **Prioritise your future manager** — a great manager compounds your growth; a bad one stalls it.
- **Consider the 3-year version of yourself** — which offer puts you in the strongest position in 2025, not just next month?
- **Do not be afraid to walk away** — no offer is better than the wrong offer.
- **Use data, not just feelings** — but trust your instincts when the data is close.
